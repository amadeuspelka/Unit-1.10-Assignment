Organic Pollutant Awareness Assignment: Bisphenol A (BPA)

Identification Functional Groups Source Properties Impact Safety Issues Disposal Issues Catholic Perspective

Identification of the Organic Compound

The organic pollutant chosen is Bisphenol A (BPA). Its chemical formula is C15H16O2. BPA is a synthetic organic compound used primarily in the production of polycarbonate plastics and epoxy resins, found in many consumer products.

Molecular Structure:

            OH - Benzene - C - Benzene - OH
                   |  |
                  CH3 CH3

The structure shows two benzene rings connected by a central carbon (C) with two methyl groups (CH3), each benzene ring having an OH group at the top.

Functional Groups

BPA contains the following functional groups, which define its chemical behavior:

  • Phenol groups (-OH): Two hydroxyl groups attached to aromatic benzene rings, enabling polymer formation.
  • Aromatic rings: Two benzene rings, providing structural stability.
  • Alkyl group: A central carbon atom bonded to two methyl groups (-CH3), contributing to hydrophobicity.

Source of the Pollutant

BPA is found in:

  • Polycarbonate plastics: Used in water bottles, food containers, and medical devices.
  • Epoxy resins: Lining of food cans, bottle tops, and water pipes.
  • Thermal paper: Receipts and tickets.
  • BPA enters the environment through manufacturing, leaching from products, and improper disposal.

Chemical and Physical Properties

BPA belongs to the bisphenol family. Key properties include:

  • Molecular Weight: 228.29 g/mol
  • Melting Point: 158–159°C
  • Boiling Point: 360°C
  • Solubility: Poorly soluble in water (120–300 mg/L at 25°C), soluble in organic solvents.
  • Stability: Stable under normal conditions but leaches when heated or exposed to acidic/alkaline conditions.

Impact on Health, Society, and the Environment

Health: BPA is an endocrine disruptor, mimicking estrogen, potentially causing reproductive issues, increased risk of cancers (e.g., breast, prostate), developmental problems in children, hormonal imbalances affecting metabolism, potential links to obesity and diabetes with prolonged exposure, and possible immune system disruptions.

Society: Public concern has led to bans (e.g., baby bottles in Canada, EU) and a shift toward BPA-free products, with growing consumer demand for safer materials influencing industry standards, prompting research into alternative chemicals, and sparking debates on regulatory policies.

Environment: BPA persists in soil and water, disrupting aquatic life, bioaccumulating in ecosystems, contributing to long-term contamination of natural habitats, posing risks to wildlife reproduction and biodiversity, and affecting the food chain through contaminated water sources.

Safety Issues (WHMIS)

Under WHMIS, BPA is classified as:

  • Hazard Class: Skin and eye irritant; potential reproductive toxin.
  • Handling Precautions: Use gloves, eye protection, and ventilation in industrial settings.
  • Labeling: Containers require hazard symbols for health risks.

Disposal Issues

Improper disposal contributes to environmental pollution. Recommendations include:

  • Recycling: Use designated programs for plastics to prevent leaching.
  • Incineration: Controlled high-temperature incineration to minimize byproducts.
  • Avoid Landfills: BPA can contaminate groundwater.

Catholic Perspective on Environmental Impact

From a Catholic perspective, BPA’s environmental harm conflicts with stewardship of God’s creation, according to the Vatican. Pollution from BPA endangers ecosystems and human health, urging Catholics to advocate for sustainable practices, reduce harmful chemical use, and promote ecological justice. This aligns with the call to protect the poor and vulnerable, who are disproportionately affected by pollution, and to foster a sense of communal responsibility to preserve the Earth for future generations. Prayer and education within parishes can also inspire action against such environmental threats.
